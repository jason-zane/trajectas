## 1. What Makes a Good Construct Definition

A construct definition is ready for item writing when it meets these four criteria:

| Criterion | Test | Failure example |
|---|---|---|
| **Specific** | Could two independent item writers produce similar items from this definition? | "Drives results" — too broad |
| **Observable** | Does the definition reference things that can be seen, heard, or verified? | "Has a growth mindset" — internal state |
| **Bounded** | Is it clear where this construct ends and another begins? | "Communication" — overlaps with influence, coaching, negotiation |
| **Level-appropriate** | Does the definition match the intended measurement level (trait/competency/SJT)? | A trait definition written in behavioural terms, or a competency defined as a personality adjective |

If a definition fails any test, refine it before proceeding.

## 2. The "Is It Measurable?" Decision Framework

Many constructs submitted for item development are not actually measurable as presented. Classify what the user has provided:

**It is a capability** if: someone can do it, can learn to do better at it, and it is demonstrated through behaviour. → Route to competency items.

**It is a trait** if: it is a stable disposition, consistent across contexts, and predicts behaviour rather than being the behaviour. → Route to trait items.

**It is something else if:**

| What it actually is | How to recognise it | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Personality trait mislabelled as competency** | Uses adjectives rather than verbs. Stable, not developable. | Redirect to trait-level measurement, or redefine as the behaviours the trait produces. |
| **Value or belief** | Describes what someone thinks is important, not what they do. | Reframe as observable behaviours that demonstrate the value. |
| **Outcome** | Describes a result, not the behaviours that produce it. | Identify the underlying capability or trait. |
| **Role description** | Describes responsibility, not skill. | Extract the actual capability underneath. |
| **Composite** | Bundles multiple distinct capabilities under one label. | Decompose and choose one facet. |
| **Attitude** | Evaluative judgement about a specific object or domain. Can shift with experience. | Reframe as the enduring disposition (trait) or the behaviour (competency) that the attitude reflects. |

**Decision tree:**
1. Can someone get better at it through practice? → If no, likely a trait. Route accordingly.
2. Does it describe what someone does or what they believe? → If belief, reframe.
3. Does it describe an outcome or a process? → If outcome, identify the underlying construct.
4. Does it contain multiple distinct skills? → If yes, decompose.
5. Could a colleague observe it? → If no, too abstract. Anchor to indicators.
6. Would it change substantially after a single training programme? → If yes, it is a state or attitude, not a trait.

## 3. Construct Boundary Analysis

Every construct exists alongside related constructs. Failure to define boundaries produces items that measure the wrong thing (contamination) or scales that correlate so highly with other scales they add nothing (redundancy).

**Common overlap patterns:**

| Construct | Confused with | Key differentiator |
|---|---|---|
| Strategic thinking | Analytical thinking | Strategic = future-oriented, direction. Analytical = present-oriented, data. |
| Influence | Communication | Influence = changing position/behaviour. Communication = transmitting information. |
| Coaching | Feedback | Coaching = developing capability over time. Feedback = information at a point in time. |
| Collaboration | Agreeableness | Collaboration = working effectively including navigating disagreement. Agreeableness = prioritising harmony. |
| Adaptability | Compliance | Adaptability = adjusting while maintaining effectiveness. Compliance = doing what is asked. |
| Decision-making | Decisiveness | Decision-making = process quality. Decisiveness = speed and commitment. |
| Innovation | Creativity | Innovation = implementing new approaches. Creativity = generating ideas. |

**For any construct, identify:**
1. The 2-3 most adjacent constructs likely to contaminate items
2. The discriminating behaviours — what someone high on this construct does that someone high on the adjacent construct might not
3. A boundary statement — one clear sentence defining what this construct is *not*

## 4. Behavioural Indicator Development (Competency and SJT Items)

Behavioural indicators bridge the construct definition and the items. Every competency or SJT item should trace back to an indicator.

**Rules for good indicators:**
- **Observable:** Could a colleague verify this behaviour?
- **Specific:** "Checks understanding after delivering complex information" not "communicates effectively"
- **Non-redundant:** Each indicator captures a distinct facet
- **Comprehensive:** Cover the full scope using difficulty tiers (foundation, applied, demanding)
- **Neutral in tone:** Describe behaviours, don't evaluate them

**Template:**

| # | Behavioural indicator | Difficulty tier | Boundary notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | [Observable behaviour] | Foundation / Applied / Demanding | [Contamination concerns] |

Generate 4-8 indicators per construct.

## 5. Trait Facet Identification (Trait Items)

For trait constructs, identify the distinct facets of the disposition rather than behavioural indicators. Facets are the sub-dimensions of the trait that items will tap.

**For each trait, identify:**
1. 3-5 distinct facets that together define the full trait
2. How each facet connects to the predicted competency (criterion linkage)
3. Which Big Five domain(s) each facet neighbours

**Example — Exploratory Mindset:**

| Facet | Description | Big Five neighbourhood | Criterion link |
|---|---|---|---|
| Novelty-seeking | Drawn to unfamiliar methods and tools | Openness — intellectual curiosity | Predicts willingness to try new AI tools |
| Persistence through difficulty | Continues engaging when initial attempts fail | Conscientiousness — persistence | Predicts iteration behaviour |
| Self-initiated exploration | Explores without external prompting | Openness + low Agreeableness (independence) | Predicts autonomous experimentation |

## 6. Common Construct Problems

Check for these known failure modes:

| Problem | Description | Signal | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Jingle fallacy** | Same name, different meanings | "We define leadership differently here" | Specify exactly; consider renaming |
| **Jangle fallacy** | Different names, same construct | Separate scales with overlapping indicators | Test overlap; merge or differentiate |
| **Kitchen sink** | Definition includes too much | Multiple capabilities joined by "and" | Decompose or choose one facet |
| **Aspirational definition** | Describes what the org wishes, not what can be measured | Value-laden, mission-statement tone | Rewrite around observable behaviours |
| **Ceiling compression** | Everyone in target population would score highly | Behaviours that are basic job requirements | Raise difficulty level; add demanding indicators |
| **Cultural specificity** | One cultural norm treated as universal | "Speaks up assertively" | Identify underlying capability; write culturally flexible indicators |
| **Positional confound** | Behaviours require specific role authority | "Sets strategic direction for the organisation" | Rewrite for target population's authority level |

## 7. Construct Refinement Protocol

When a definition is weak, do not just flag the problem. Follow this structure:

1. State the specific problem with the current definition
2. Propose a revised definition
3. Explain what changed and why
4. Present updated indicators/facets that flow from the revision
5. Ask the user to confirm before proceeding

## 8. Red Flags That Halt Item Writing

### For competency constructs — do not proceed if:
- The definition contains 3+ distinct capabilities joined by "and"
- The indicators could equally apply to a different named competency
- The target population cannot demonstrate the behaviours (positional confound)
- The definition is a rephrased value rather than a measurable capability
- Two competencies in the same assessment share >30% of indicators

### For trait constructs — do not proceed if:
- The construct would change substantially after a single training programme
- The construct is an attitude about a specific technology or domain
- The construct maps cleanly to an existing Big Five facet with no differentiation
- No downstream competency has been identified as a prediction criterion
- The construct is a value statement rather than a behavioural tendency
- The construct is described in situational/behavioural terms (may belong at competency level)

### For SJT constructs — do not proceed if:
- The construct cannot be expressed as a decision or judgement scenario
- The scenarios would have one obviously correct answer (no genuine trade-off)
- The target population would not plausibly encounter the scenarios
- The construct is better measured by self-report (simple frequency or preference)
