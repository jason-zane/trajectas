# Trajectas — Strategic Review v2 (Competitive Edition)

*Date: 29 April 2026. Companion to v1 (`strategic-review-2026-04-29.md`). This version replaces the strategic recommendation with one informed by deep competitor research.*

---

## What changed since v1

The v1 review concluded that the right strategic direction was Fork B (bridge hiring → development) for the right reason (longitudinal capability data is the only moat that compounds). After deep competitive research, **the direction is the same but the urgency, sequencing, and competitive posture all change**. There is one piece of news that materially shifts the picture:

> **Phenom acquired Plum.** Phenom now has psychometric assessment science *plus* enterprise distribution *plus* $169M of capital (with a $100M Series D already done). Their stated rationale is to "validate durable skills AI can't replace" — emotional intelligence, adaptability, judgment, resilience. That is verbatim Trajectas's thesis being executed by a much larger, much better-capitalised competitor.

This isn't a death sentence. It's a validation of the thesis and a signal that the window to claim the productised-contextual-competency wedge is *narrower than it looked*. Phenom is enterprise-first; they will move slowly into mid-market and they will not productise an org diagnostic the way Trajectas can. But they will eventually claim "we have psychometric assessment + AI delivery + lifecycle data," and after that point the story Trajectas tells gets harder to tell.

Two other moves matter:

- **Workday acquired Sana Labs (~$1.1B, late 2025) and HiredScore (2024).** Workday is assembling a bundled AI-driven talent stack: HRIS + recruiting + learning + skills inference. They don't have measurement rigour. They're betting on adoption velocity over proven efficacy.
- **Eightfold raised a $220M Series E in 2025 (SoftBank-led, $2.1B valuation).** They have $96.6M ARR and 100 enterprise customers. Their skills graph is largely keyword-matching with NLP overlays — no published predictive validity. They are vulnerable to a measurement-validity attack but they have distribution Trajectas does not.

The competitive landscape now bifurcates clearly into two tiers:
- **Infrastructure tier**: skills graphs, ATS integration, bias detection. Commodity; winners will be Eightfold, Workday, Phenom by distribution.
- **Capability measurement tier**: validated competency assessment, predictive validity, longitudinal data. Undersupplied, defensible, legally meaningful.

Trajectas belongs in the second tier. So does Phenom-Plum. Almost no one else.

## The competitive landscape, abbreviated

I researched ~30 competitors across four segments. The condensed map:

**Hiring assessment incumbents.** Plum, Hogan, SHL, Predictive Index, Pymetrics (Harver), Korn Ferry Assess, Criteria, HireVue, TestGorilla, AssessFirst, Bryq. Pricing for mid-market hiring assessment programmes ranges roughly $20–250K/year depending on volume. Most sell off-the-shelf libraries; *none* sell genuinely bespoke contextual competency models out of the box. AI-driven item generation is moving from differentiator to table stakes — Plum, HireVue and Bryq have publicly shipped AI features in 2024–2026; SHL and Hogan have been slower. Validation rigour varies wildly: Hogan and SHL publish actual studies; many newer entrants don't. Pymetrics→Harver was the major recent consolidation.

**Talent development & engagement platforms.** Lattice, Culture Amp, 15Five, Leapsome, Betterworks, Cornerstone, Workday Talent, SuccessFactors. Pricing is $5–15/employee/month. Almost none have any psychometric backbone — they collect manager ratings, peer feedback, and self-report, all of which are noisy and unvalidated. The "skills" features in Lattice and Culture Amp are basically tagged competency lists with no measurement. **No incumbent in this segment has a validated longitudinal capability log on identified employees.** That is genuinely white space.

**Talent intelligence / skills graph platforms.** Eightfold AI, Gloat, Beamery, TechWolf, iCIMS, Workday Skills Cloud. These claim "AI skills inference" from work history, job descriptions, and behavioural signals. Pricing is $50–200/employee/year. The blunt truth: their accuracy claims are *label-recognition* accuracy ("we correctly tagged this person as having Python skill"), not *construct validity* ("our score predicts on-the-job performance"). Predictive validity studies are rare or absent. Industrial-organisational psychology literature in 2025 increasingly argues these skills graphs are **inferred labels without proven construct validity** — a category of risk that hasn't priced in yet but will.

**Coaching at scale.** BetterUp, CoachHub, EZRA, Bravely. Premium-priced ($200–600/employee/year), coach-network model, no rigorous capability measurement. Adjacent rather than competing.

**Org diagnostic / culture assessment.** McKinsey OHI, Bain Founder's Mentality, BCG, Korn Ferry, Heidrick, Mercer Sirota dominate enterprise (each typically $50–150K diagnostic + $500K–5M follow-on). Productised mid-market is fragmented: Culture Amp does engagement diagnostics, Microsoft Viva (formerly Glint), Workday Peakon, Energage, Humu, Quantum Workplace. **No productised platform credibly links org diagnostic → contextual competency selection.** That is also white space.

**AI-native specifically.** The headline acquisitions: Phenom→Plum, Workday→Sana Labs, Workday→HiredScore. Anthropic and OpenAI are largely silent on HR-tech; OpenAI has signalled an "AI-powered jobs platform" launch by mid-2026 but it's a marketplace play, not an assessment play. The dominant 2025–2026 IO-psychology research consensus is that AI-generated assessment items can pass internal-consistency tests but **construct validity is under-tested and predictive validity is almost never validated**.

## Where this leaves Trajectas — the sharpened thesis

Two pieces of white space are real and defensible. **Both** of them have to anchor the strategy:

**White space 1 — Productised org diagnostic that drives contextual competency selection.** McKinsey, Bain, BCG own this conceptually but cannot productise without cannibalising consulting revenue. Culture Amp and Viva diagnose engagement, not culture-as-operating-style. No one connects the diagnostic output to a competency model that hiring and development hang off. This is the *wedge*.

**White space 2 — Validated longitudinal capability data on identified employees.** Lattice/15Five/Culture Amp collect noisy ratings. Eightfold/Gloat infer skills as labels. BetterUp coaches but doesn't measure. No one has measured the same person against the same calibrated competency framework over years and tied that to business outcomes. This is the *moat*.

These two together produce the strategy:

> **Trajectas is the productised org-diagnostic and contextual-competency platform for mid-market organisations that can't afford McKinsey, sold via consulting partners. The wedge is hiring; the recurring revenue is longitudinal development tracking; the moat is the accumulated, validated longitudinal data — published and defensible.**

Three decisions follow from this:

1. **Mid-market is the right ICP, not enterprise.** Phenom and Workday will fight for Fortune 500. McKinsey owns the F500 culture diagnostic. Going up against any of them is a losing battle. The ~250–2,500-person mid-market segment cannot afford McKinsey, doesn't fit Workday's enterprise sales motion, and is *huge*. AssessFirst, Plum, and Predictive Index have proved this segment buys assessments; nobody has proved it buys contextual diagnostics yet, but the budget is there.

2. **Consulting partners are the right channel, not direct sales.** Consultants are a force multiplier in mid-market: they have relationships, they sell change as a story rather than a feature comparison, and they extract value from contextualisation that direct sales finds hard to articulate. The 3-tier (Platform → Partners → Clients) architecture you've already built is the right shape for this.

3. **Published validity evidence is the strategic asset.** Phenom, Workday, Eightfold can outspend Trajectas on engineering and sales forever. They cannot easily out-publish Trajectas on peer-reviewed predictive validity, IRT invariance, and cross-organisation construct generalisation — because their products aren't designed to support that kind of study. Trajectas's psychometric backbone is. Lean into it; turn it into the credibility moat that no amount of capital buys.

## The value-prioritised roadmap

The user's instruction was: prioritise by value, then annotate with build state. Here it is. Each item is rated by **strategic value** (1=existential, 2=core, 3=important, 4=nice-to-have) and the build state is shown at the end of each line: **[BUILT]**, **[HALF]**, **[NOT BUILT]**, **[STUBBED]**, or **[CONCEPTUAL]** (schema or design exists but no live implementation).

### Tier 1 — Existential. Without these you don't have a product.

1. **Org diagnostic that produces a defensible culture/operating-style profile.** The wedge product. Multi-layer, anonymity-preserving, role-rep ready. **[HALF]** — schema and architecture shipped 20 April; full launch flow, respondent UX, admin UX, and the actual diagnostic output rendering still in progress per your note.
2. **Matching algorithm: org diagnostic → contextual competency selection.** The thing that turns a culture profile into a recommended competency set per role. This is the single feature that justifies the entire product over a generic competency library. **[CONCEPTUAL]** — `org_diagnostic_profiles` table exists; the algorithm itself is not implemented or is mostly conceptual.
3. **LLM wiring (production).** Real Claude/OpenAI calls for item generation, AI narrative, preflight construct analysis. Currently mocked. Without this, the AI-GENIE pipeline is a diagram. **[STUBBED]** — architecture in place, calls are stubs.
4. **Competency library with the Dimensions → Factors → Constructs → Items hierarchy.** **[BUILT]** — flexible-taxonomy work landed in April; this is solid.
5. **Psychometric backbone (IRT 1PL/2PL/3PL, CTT, calibration, norm groups, DIF).** The science credibility moat. **[BUILT]** — this is genuinely an asset; almost no competitor in your tier has it.
6. **Assessment runner that delivers calibrated assessments to participants.** **[BUILT]** — the participant + session model is working; recent specs around runner performance and seeding suggest active iteration.
7. **Report generation pipeline.** Snapshot lifecycle, band schemes, derived + AI narrative, PDF rendering. **[BUILT]** — multiple recent specs indicate this is mature.
8. **Multi-tenant 3-tier model (Platform → Partners → Clients).** Required for the partner-channel strategy. **[BUILT]** — RLS, entitlements, partner branding, all present.

### Tier 2 — Core moat. These are what take Trajectas from "another assessment tool" to "a defensible business."

9. **Stable Person identity that survives across hiring → onboarding → development cycles.** The spine of the longitudinal data moat. Today the data model is campaign-centric; this is the single most important architectural shift. **[NOT BUILT]** — would be a small additive migration plus a meaningful identity-resolution policy.
10. **Longitudinal capability log (append-only, per person, per competency, with statistical metadata).** The actual moat. Every measurement event from any campaign writes here. **[NOT BUILT]**.
11. **Pulse measurement runner.** Short (8–12 calibrated items) repeatable assessments that share the IRT scale of the full instrument. The mechanism by which longitudinal data gets cheap enough to collect quarterly. **[NOT BUILT]** — but the assessment runner and IRT scoring make this much smaller than it looks.
12. **Manager-facing development view.** Team capability dashboard, individual growth view, suggested development conversations. **The single most important new surface to build.** Without this there is no buyer for development tracking. **[NOT BUILT]**.
13. **Predictive validity evidence — the first study.** A published or publishable analysis showing that Trajectas competency scores at hire predict 6–12 month outcomes (productivity, retention, manager ratings, internal mobility) at one or more partner orgs. This is the credibility moat against Eightfold/Phenom/Workday. **[NOT BUILT]** — this is research work, not engineering, but it needs an internal owner.
14. **Consent / retention / privacy compliance layer for longitudinal data.** Explicit consent, retention windows, right-to-be-forgotten, role-based access, audit trails. GDPR Article 22, EEOC, EU works-council. **Build this before customer data accumulates, not after.** **[NOT BUILT]**.
15. **Anonymity-vs-identified mode separation in the data model.** Org diagnostic must remain anonymous; development tracking must be identified. Today the line is implicit; it needs to be explicit, enforced, and visible in the UX. **[HALF]** — RLS for diagnostic anonymity is built; the mode separation as a first-class concept is not.

### Tier 3 — Important. These convert wedge into a real annual-recurring business.

16. **HRIS integrations (BambooHR / HiBob / Workday / Rippling / Personio).** Lifecycle event anchoring (hire, role change, promotion, exit) is what keeps the longitudinal data interpretable. **[NOT BUILT]**.
17. **Change-score statistics + interpretation in plain English.** "Mary's 'judgment under uncertainty' has improved with statistical significance over 12 months" — managers need this in language they can act on. **[CONCEPTUAL]** — IRT framework supports it; the surfacing layer doesn't exist.
18. **Cohort / team capability heatmaps.** Group views over the longitudinal log. **[NOT BUILT]**.
19. **Crosswalk to industry-standard competency models (SHL, Hogan, KF4D).** Some buyers want benchmarking against the standards. The crosswalk lets Trajectas have both bespoke *and* benchmark — a sales objection demolisher. **[NOT BUILT]**.
20. **Person-facing self-service view.** "My capability profile, my growth, my recommendations." Drives manager + employee both seeing the value, increases retention, and is also a plausible viral surface. **[NOT BUILT]**.
21. **Email and notification production wiring.** Reminders, results-ready notices, manager nudges, pulse invitations. Skeletal today. **[STUBBED]**.
22. **Partner branding, entitlements, marketing site.** Channel enablement. **[BUILT/HALF]** — multiple specs in March-April have addressed branding and entitlements; assume most of this is functional.
23. **Score interpretation layer (v2).** Scoring → interpretation → action. **[HALF]** — recent spec (`2026-04-17-score-interpretation-v2-design.md`) suggests this is in flight.
24. **Participant comparison views.** Cross-participant analytics. **[HALF]** — spec dated 27 April; in flight.

### Tier 4 — Differentiators that compound the moat.

25. **Manager workflow embedding (Slack/Teams nudges, calendar integration, 1:1 prep templates).** Adoption multiplier. The difference between "a thing managers log into quarterly" and "a thing that quietly makes them better managers." **[NOT BUILT]**.
26. **Cross-org benchmarking (anonymised, opt-in).** A defensible data product that improves with every customer. **[NOT BUILT]** — but it's a downstream feature of having longitudinal data across many orgs.
27. **Intervention efficacy tracking.** "Which development actions actually move the score?" The analytic that justifies the platform's existence to a CFO. **[NOT BUILT]**.
28. **Vertical overlays (engineering, sales, healthcare, financial services).** Sector-specific competency benchmarks once you have enough longitudinal data. **[NOT BUILT]**.
29. **Adaptive / CAT runtime sequencing.** The infrastructure exists; full CAT delivery doesn't. Save it until pulse cadence and participation rates make it worthwhile. **[HALF]**.

### Tier 5 — Strategic optionality. Not now, but watch.

30. **API-first capability data product (Fork D).** Selling competency models / benchmarks to other HR tools. Worth picking up only after you have a critical mass of longitudinal data. Don't build until 2027. **[NOT BUILT]**.
31. **AI-native competency inference from observable work signals (Fork E2).** This is the 2027–2028 product if longitudinal data gets you good enough labelled training data. Today's "skills inference" platforms (TechWolf, Eightfold) are doing this badly; doing it well requires an anchored measurement layer (which Trajectas would have). **[NOT BUILT]**.
32. **Marketplace for partner-built assessments.** Network-effect feature once you have 30+ partners. **[NOT BUILT]**.
33. **Acquisition / OpenAI-jobs integration.** OpenAI's mid-2026 jobs platform launch is worth tracking; it could be an integration channel rather than a competitor. **[N/A]**.

## Pricing and ICP, anchored to the competitive data

Based on the competitive pricing data:

- **Hiring-only positioning** (Fork A, what you have today): mid-market deals likely $30–80K/year per client. Margins are healthy but the ceiling is bounded by the size of the hiring assessment market and the fact that you'd be a feature in someone else's stack.
- **Hiring + development positioning** (Fork B, recommended): per-employee-per-year pricing of roughly $80–180 PEPY for mid-market, blended across both halves. For a 500-person client at $130 PEPY blended, that's $65K/year, with growth as the longitudinal product gets used. For a 1,500-person client, $195K/year — competitive with Lattice, BetterUp, and below Workday Talent. Phenom and Eightfold sit higher (typically $200K+ floor).
- **Channel economics**: typical consulting-partner economics in this category give the partner 25–40% margin on the platform fee, with the partner adding their own services overlay (typically 1–2× the platform fee). This means a £100K platform deal ends up looking like a £200–300K total client engagement, which is a far easier conversation for partners to have than "this is a £100K SaaS line item."

The right ICP for the recommended strategy is:
- **Size**: 250–2,500 employees.
- **Stage**: post-product-market-fit, hiring deliberately, has at least one of (CHRO, VP Talent, Head of People).
- **Geography**: UK and Western Europe first (where AssessFirst, Talogy, and Bryq are live but where contextual diagnostic positioning is genuinely under-served), then North America.
- **Buyer**: VP People / Head of Talent. Avoid pitching CHROs of orgs that have a Workday or SuccessFactors HCM unless you're explicitly positioning as an overlay.
- **Pain**: "We're hiring for fit and we don't know what fit is here, and we're investing in development without knowing what's working."

## The competitor watchlist (in priority order)

1. **Phenom + Plum.** Closest direct threat. They will productise psychometric assessment + AI delivery within 12 months. Trajectas's defence is mid-market focus + productised org diagnostic + partner channel — none of which Phenom has. Watch their product announcements monthly.

2. **Workday's bundled AI talent stack.** Workday + Sana + HiredScore is consolidating, and Workday is the natural acquirer if Trajectas builds the right moat. Watch for Workday integration announcements with assessment vendors and for any explicit Workday move into validated assessment.

3. **Eightfold AI.** Will not threaten directly on validation any time soon, but their distribution and capital make them an existential risk if they ever publish credible predictive validity studies. Track their academic/research output.

4. **Lattice and Culture Amp.** Either could add a cheap competency-tracking feature that is "good enough" for mid-market buyers who don't care about validity. The defence is the validity narrative — keep publishing.

5. **OpenAI Jobs platform (mid-2026 launch).** Most likely a marketplace, not an assessment tool. Could be an integration partner. Watch the launch carefully.

6. **Anthropic.** No HR-tech moves yet, but worth monitoring hiring patterns. If Anthropic launched validated assessment APIs, Trajectas's psychometric depth would be the only defensible moat. Publish evidence early to claim the science narrative.

7. **A new well-capitalised entrant combining UX + measurement science + AI.** Lattice-style UX, Eightfold-style AI infrastructure, and a real psychometrician on the cap table. Plausible 2026–2027 entrant. Trajectas's defence is having shipped first.

## The strong steer

If this review needs one paragraph, it's this:

> The thesis is right. The window is real but narrowing. **Don't try to compete with Phenom, Workday, or Eightfold on capital, distribution, or breadth.** Compete on three things they can't replicate cheaply: (1) the *productised org diagnostic* that turns culture into contextual competencies — McKinsey-quality output at mid-market pricing, sold via consulting partners; (2) the *validated longitudinal capability data* on identified employees — measured, not inferred, accumulated over years, defensible in published research; (3) the *psychometric science credibility* — IRT, calibration, predictive validity studies — published openly so it's the public-record version of the moat. The first one is your wedge. The second one is your moat. The third one is your defence against everything that's better-capitalised.

If you are not willing to play the slow research-credibility game (publish predictive validity studies, talk at IO-psychology conferences, get cited in HR-tech analyst reports), the strategy collapses and you are just another assessment startup with a clever schema. If you *are* willing, this is a venture-scale, defensible business with a 3–5 year window before consolidation forces a choice between Series B and acquisition — and either of those is a good outcome.

The single most important thing to do this quarter is finish the wedge (matching algorithm, LLM wiring, end-to-end org diagnostic) and run the Phase 0 validation experiment from v1. The single most important hiring or partnership to make this year is finding the IO psychologist or research lead who will own the predictive validity programme. That role is the moat-builder; without it, the moat doesn't get built no matter how much you ship.

## Open questions / things I'm uncertain about

- **Is mid-market contextual-diagnostic pricing real?** The competitive data shows mid-market buys hiring assessments at $20–250K and engagement platforms at $5–15 PEPY, but no one has actually proven mid-market buys *productised diagnostics* in volume. The Phase 0 experiment partly answers this; deeper buyer-discovery interviews would help.
- **Phenom's mid-market intent.** I assume Phenom will stay enterprise-focused for the next 18 months because that's where their motion is, but if they pivot to mid-market via a smaller-deal motion or partner channel, the competitive picture changes meaningfully.
- **Pricing per employee for development tracking specifically.** Lattice/Culture Amp at $5–15 PEPY is the floor; BetterUp at $300–600 PEPY is the ceiling. The "validated longitudinal capability" position probably justifies $50–150 PEPY but I'd want pricing-discovery interviews before committing.
- **Whether Trajectas can credibly publish research.** Publishing in IO-psychology journals takes time, peer review, and credibility signals (academic co-authors, conference presentations). It's the moat but it's not free. Worth scoping early.
- **Regulatory exposure in EU vs US.** GDPR Article 22 around automated decision-making is a real constraint on any product that uses assessment data for hiring or promotion decisions. Will need actual legal counsel before the longitudinal product launches.
