# Competency Item Design Guide

Read this document when the user is building competency-level (observable behaviour) items. These items measure what someone currently does in the workplace — developable capabilities demonstrated through action.

## 1. What Competency Measurement Is

Competency items measure **observable, developable workplace behaviours** — what someone actually does, not who they are. They are downstream from traits: how dispositions manifest in practice.

**Use competency items when the goal is:**
- Diagnosing current capability level
- Identifying development needs
- Measuring behavioural change over time
- Providing feedback on specific workplace skills

Competency items are tied to context. They reference real work situations, specific actions, and observable outcomes. A well-informed colleague should be able to independently rate the respondent on a good competency item.

---

## 2. Competency Construct Inputs Checklist

Collect all of the following before proceeding. Ask all questions together.

- **Competency name**
- **Working definition** — must be observable and behavioural
- **Target population** (and seniority level — this affects anti-inflation requirements)
- **Context of use** (development, feedback, selection, 360)
- **Measurement focus:** behavioural consistency, confidence, frequency, or judgement
- **Desired final item count**
- **Reverse-keyed items** (yes/no)
- **Social desirability detection items** (yes/no)
- **Item context:** general or work-specific
- **Target reading level** (plain language / standard / graduate-executive)

Do not proceed until inputs are confirmed by the user.

---

## 3. Competency-Specific Construct Analysis

In addition to the general construct analysis (construct-analysis.md), verify:

1. **The definition passes the observability test:** Could a colleague rate someone on this without reading their mind?
2. **The definition passes the developability test:** Could someone improve through training and practice? If not, it may be a trait.
3. **Behavioural indicators are generated** using the template in the construct analysis guide (4-8 indicators spanning foundation, applied, demanding difficulty).

## 4. Item Writing Rules

### Rule 1: Behavioural Focus

Every item must reference observable behaviour, decisions, or responses to situations.

**The verification test:** Could a well-informed colleague independently rate the respondent on this item?

| Bad (self-concept / internal state) | Good (observable behaviour) |
|---|---|
| I am a strategic thinker | I connect my day-to-day decisions to our longer-term goals |
| I have high emotional intelligence | I notice when a team member's behaviour changes and check in with them |
| I am passionate about innovation | I regularly propose new approaches to existing processes |
| I value diversity | I actively seek input from people with different perspectives before making decisions |
| I am resilient | I maintain my focus on priorities after receiving setbacks or criticism |

### Rule 2: No Virtue Signalling

Avoid items that describe a desirable identity. These measure self-regard, not capability.

**Signals of virtue signalling:**
- Uses "I am a..." or "I am someone who..."
- Describes a quality rather than an action
- Would be embarrassing to disagree with
- Sounds like a job interview answer

| Virtue signalling | Behavioural alternative |
|---|---|
| I am an ethical leader | I raise concerns when I see decisions that conflict with stated policies, even when uncomfortable |
| I am a great communicator | I check that my message has been understood before moving on |
| I lead by example | I hold myself to the same standards I set for my team |

### Rule 3: No Extreme Language

Avoid: always, never, constantly, perfectly, every single time, without exception.

Extreme language causes honest respondents to underrate themselves and means only fakers endorse the item. Exception: social desirability detection items use extreme language deliberately.

### Rule 4: Single Idea Per Item

Each item measures exactly one behaviour. Double-barrelled items are uninterpretable.

**Detection:** The item contains "and" linking two distinct actions, or would require two different ratings if split.

| Double-barrelled | Split alternatives |
|---|---|
| I communicate clearly and listen actively | "I structure my messages so the key point is easy to identify" / "I listen to understand the other person's position before responding" |
| I set ambitious goals and support my team to achieve them | "I set goals that stretch beyond current performance levels" / "I help team members identify what they need to achieve their goals" |
| I seek feedback and act on it | "I ask for specific feedback on my work" / "I make concrete changes based on feedback I receive" |

### Rule 5: No Obvious Answers

**The transparency test:** Would someone who has never demonstrated this capability know exactly which answer to pick? If yes, rewrite.

**Strategies:**

| Strategy | Example |
|---|---|
| Add behavioural specificity | "I identify what information is missing before making a decision" |
| Add situational complexity | "When a team member is underperforming, I address it directly rather than working around them" |
| Add cost or trade-off | "I seek out feedback even when I suspect it will be critical" |
| Use reverse-keying | "I sometimes move forward with a decision before fully considering alternatives" |

### Rule 6: Vary Behavioural Difficulty

Items should span three tiers:

| Tier | What it captures | Example (delegation) |
|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** (20-30%) | Basic capability most competent people demonstrate | "I assign tasks based on team members' strengths and development needs" |
| **Applied** (40-50%) | Consistent application under normal conditions | "I provide clear context and expected outcomes when delegating, rather than just assigning tasks" |
| **Demanding** (20-30%) | Maintained under pressure, ambiguity, or competing priorities | "I delegate important tasks even when uncertain the person will do it exactly as I would, and manage my discomfort with that" |

### Rule 7: Prefer Frequency/Consistency Over Agreement Framing

Agreement framing ("I believe X is important") measures attitudes. Frequency/consistency framing measures behaviour.

| Agreement (avoid) | Frequency/consistency (prefer) |
|---|---|
| I believe feedback is important for development | I provide specific, actionable feedback to my team at least weekly |
| I think it is important to plan ahead | I review and adjust my priorities at the start of each week |

### Rule 8: Reading Level Adjustment

| Target | Characteristics | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Plain language** | Short sentences, common words, concrete | "I ask questions when I do not understand something" |
| **Standard** | Moderate complexity, workplace terminology | "I seek clarification on ambiguous requirements before committing" |
| **Graduate/executive** | Nuanced framing, conditional structures | "When facing competing strategic priorities, I evaluate trade-offs against our longer-term positioning before committing resources" |

General rules: max one subordinate clause per item; avoid passive voice; avoid nominalisation; keep under 25 words where possible, never exceed 35.

### Rule 9: Cultural Sensitivity

Avoid encoding one cultural norm as "correct." Check for assumptions about speaking up, individual initiative, direct feedback, and speed of decision-making.

| Assumption | Problem | Culturally flexible alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Speaking up is good | Some cultures value indirect communication | "I ensure my perspective is factored into important decisions" (method-neutral) |
| Individual initiative is good | Collectivist cultures prioritise group harmony | "I contribute to moving work forward, whether by taking initiative or supporting others' efforts" |
| Direct feedback is good | Some cultures view direct negative feedback as disrespectful | "I communicate performance expectations clearly and address gaps in a way that maintains the relationship" |

## 5. Anti-Inflation Techniques (Mandatory for Senior Leader Populations)

Self-enhancement is the biggest measurement threat in senior leader self-report. Leaders believe they are effective, and normative leadership items are endorsed at ceiling regardless of actual capability.

**These techniques are mandatory for senior leaders/executives and high-stakes assessments. Apply them from the first draft — do not write standard items and "upgrade" later.**

| Population | Approach |
|---|---|
| General workforce | Standard item writing rules are usually sufficient |
| Middle managers | Apply friction situations and trade-off framing to at least 30% of items |
| Senior leaders / executives | Apply anti-inflation techniques to the majority of items |
| High-stakes (selection, promotion) | Apply anti-inflation techniques to all items regardless of population |

### Technique 1: Trade-Off Framing

Describe behaviours that require giving something up.

| Without trade-off (inflates) | With trade-off (discriminates) |
|---|---|
| I set clear priorities | I reduce competing priorities when teams are overloaded, even when stakeholders push back |
| I develop my people | I invest time in developing team members even when it would be faster to do the work myself |
| I make timely decisions | I make decisions with incomplete information when the situation requires it, rather than waiting for certainty |

### Technique 2: Friction Situations

Describe behaviours under pressure, conflict, or ambiguity.

| Low-friction (inflates) | High-friction (discriminates) |
|---|---|
| I give feedback to my team | When a high performer's behaviour is damaging the team, I address it directly rather than overlooking it because of their results |
| I address underperformance | When someone I personally like is underperforming, I hold the same standard I would for anyone else |
| I adapt my approach | When a strategy I championed is not working, I change course rather than persisting to avoid admitting the original approach was wrong |

### Technique 3: Behavioural Specificity Under Pressure

| Abstract (inflates) | Specific under pressure (discriminates) |
|---|---|
| I communicate effectively | When delivering unwelcome news, I communicate the rationale and expected impact clearly rather than softening the message |
| I build trust | I share information about risks and setbacks with my team rather than shielding them from bad news |
| I empower my team | I allow my team to make decisions in their domain even when I disagree with their approach |

### Technique 4: The "Honest Low-Performer" Test

For every item: Would a leader who is genuinely weak on this competency still rate themselves 4 or 5? If yes, rewrite.

### Technique 5: Conditional Behaviours

Frame items as "even when" or "rather than" statements.

| Unconditional (inflates) | Conditional (discriminates) |
|---|---|
| I follow through on commitments | I renegotiate or escalate commitments I cannot meet rather than letting them quietly slip |
| I seek feedback | I seek feedback from people likely to be critical, not only those who will affirm my approach |
| I make data-driven decisions | I change my position when data contradicts my initial view, rather than seeking confirming data |

## 6. Item Pool Format

Present every competency item pool using this structure:

| # | Item text | Indicator | Type | Key | SD risk | Difficulty | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | [Statement] | [Indicator #] | Cap/Freq/Jdg/Conf | +/R | Low/Mod/High | Found/App/Dem | [Concerns] |

**Column definitions:**
- **Indicator:** Maps to a specific behavioural indicator from construct analysis
- **Type:** Cap (capability), Freq (frequency), Jdg (judgement), Conf (confidence)
- **Key:** + (positive) or R (reverse-keyed)
- **SD risk:** Social desirability risk
- **Difficulty:** Foundation / Applied / Demanding
- **Notes:** Concerns, design rationale, flagged issues

## 7. Social Desirability Detection

Include when: high-faking-motivation context (selection, promotion), user requests them, or the construct is particularly vulnerable.

**Improbable virtue items** — desirable but almost no one does consistently:
- "I have never felt frustrated with a colleague's work"
- "I have never made a decision I later regretted"

**Unrealistic consistency items** — maintaining a desirable behaviour at an implausible level:
- "I remain completely calm in every stressful situation, without exception"
- "I give every person I work with exactly the same amount of attention and support"

**Rules:**
- 2-4 per competency, maximum
- Match tone and format of genuine items
- Avoid well-known traps ("I have never told a lie")
- Calibrate to the population — what counts as implausible depends on the target group
- SD items scored separately, never as part of the competency total

## 8. Competency-Specific Quality Review

Apply these in addition to the standard quality review (quality-review.md).

### Individual Item Checks

| Check | Question | Action if failed |
|---|---|---|
| Behavioural focus | Observable behaviour, not self-concept? | Rewrite to reference specific actions |
| Single construct | Measures only the intended competency? | Identify contamination; rewrite or cut |
| Non-obvious desirability | Could a low-capability person identify the "right" answer? | Add specificity, complexity, or cost |
| Honest low-performer test | Would a weak leader still rate 4 or 5? | Apply anti-inflation techniques |
| Authority level | Can the target population demonstrate this? | Rewrite for correct authority level |
| Cultural sensitivity | Encodes specific cultural norm as correct? | Rewrite to be method-neutral |

### Pool-Level Checks

| Check | Target |
|---|---|
| Indicator coverage | All behavioural indicators represented by 2+ items |
| Difficulty distribution | 20-30% foundation, 40-50% applied, 20-30% demanding |
| Keying balance | 20-30% reverse-keyed if requested |
| SD profile | Fewer than 30% high SD risk |
| Redundancy | No two items measuring same indicator at same difficulty |

## 9. Common Item Flaws and Fixes

| Flaw | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Self-concept | "I am a good listener" | "I summarise what I've heard before responding to check my understanding" |
| Truism | "I believe results matter" | Remove or replace with behavioural item |
| Hypothetical | "If I saw unfairness, I would speak up" | "When I see a decision I believe is unfair, I raise my concern" |
| Jargon | "I leverage synergies across the value chain" | "I look for ways to combine efforts across teams to get better results" |
| Leading preamble | "Because effective leaders delegate, I delegate tasks" | "I delegate tasks based on capabilities and development goals" |
| Positional confound | "I set the strategic direction for my organisation" | "I connect my team's work to strategic priorities" |
| Culturally loaded | "I speak up assertively in group discussions" | "I ensure my perspective is considered in group decisions" |
