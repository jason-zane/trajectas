# Situational Judgement Test (SJT) Item Design Guide

Read this document when the user is building SJT items. These items present realistic workplace scenarios and ask respondents to evaluate or choose between plausible response options, measuring judgement and decision-making quality rather than self-reported behaviour.

## 1. What SJT Measurement Is

SJTs measure **applied judgement** — how someone evaluates situations, weighs competing demands, and selects courses of action. Unlike self-report items (trait or competency), SJTs do not ask "what are you like?" or "what do you do?" They ask "what would you do in this specific situation?" or "which of these actions is most effective?"

**Why SJTs exist:** Self-report items measure self-concept and behavioural intention. SJTs measure the quality of someone's reasoning about situations, which is harder to fake and more predictive of actual decision-making behaviour.

### How SJTs Differ From Self-Report Items

| | SJT items | Trait items | Competency items |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Format** | Scenario + response options | Single statement | Single statement |
| **What they measure** | Judgement and decision quality | Stable disposition | Observable behaviour |
| **Response mode** | Choose best/worst action | Rate agreement | Rate consistency/frequency |
| **Faking resistance** | Moderate to high | Low | Low to moderate |
| **Design complexity** | High (3-5x per item) | Moderate | Moderate |
| **Scoring** | Requires expert keying or empirical validation | Endorsement direction | Endorsement direction |
| **Best for** | Selection, promotion, judgement-heavy roles | Prediction, potential identification | Development, feedback |

## 2. When to Use SJTs (and When Not To)

**SJTs are the right choice when:**
- The construct involves trade-offs, prioritisation, or navigating competing demands
- Self-report would produce highly inflated scores (very senior populations, high-stakes contexts)
- Judgement quality matters more than behavioural frequency
- The assessment is for selection or promotion where faking resistance matters
- The construct involves "knowing when to do what" rather than "doing X consistently"

**SJTs are the wrong choice when:**
- The construct is a simple, single behaviour (use competency items)
- The goal is measuring stable disposition (use trait items)
- The target population would not plausibly encounter the scenarios
- There is no genuine trade-off — one response option would be obviously correct to everyone
- The user cannot access subject matter experts for scoring validation
- Rapid assessment is needed — SJTs take significantly longer to complete than Likert scales

**SJTs can complement self-report:** In a multi-method assessment, SJTs can validate self-reported competency. If someone rates themselves highly on "strategic thinking" but performs poorly on strategic thinking SJT scenarios, the discrepancy is diagnostic.

## 3. SJT Construct Inputs Checklist

Collect all of the following before proceeding. Ask all questions together.

- **Construct name**
- **Working definition** — must describe a judgement or decision-making capability
- **Target population** (seniority, function, industry)
- **Context of use** (selection, promotion, development, calibration)
- **Scenario context:** specific industry/role, or generic professional situations
- **Response instruction format:** most/least effective, rank order, or best single choice (see Section 7)
- **Number of scenarios desired** (typically 6-12 for a single construct)
- **Number of response options per scenario** (typically 4; range 3-5)
- **Reading level** (plain language / standard / graduate-executive)
- **Whether scenarios should reference specific role contexts** (e.g. "you are a project manager") or be generic

Do not proceed until inputs are confirmed.

## 4. SJT-Specific Construct Analysis

In addition to the general construct analysis (construct-analysis.md):

### 4.1 Trade-Off Identification

The core of SJT design is identifying the real trade-offs that people in the target population face. Each scenario must present a genuine dilemma where reasonable people might disagree on the best action.

For the construct, identify 4-6 core trade-offs:

| Trade-off | Tension | Example scenario seed |
|---|---|---|
| Speed vs. thoroughness | Acting quickly on incomplete information vs. waiting for more data | A client wants an answer by end of day; you have partial analysis |
| Directness vs. relationship | Giving critical feedback vs. preserving a working relationship | A peer's work quality is slipping but they are under personal stress |
| Autonomy vs. alignment | Taking initiative vs. checking with stakeholders | You see an opportunity but your manager has not approved the approach |
| Short-term vs. long-term | Immediate deliverable vs. sustainable process | You can hit the deadline by cutting corners that will create technical debt |

Without genuine trade-offs, SJT items have obvious answers and measure nothing.

### 4.2 Scenario Plausibility Check

Every scenario must be:
- **Realistic** for the target population (they have actually faced or could face this)
- **Role-appropriate** (the respondent has the authority to take the described actions)
- **Sufficiently complex** (no single obviously correct answer)
- **Not culturally biased** (the "right" answer should not depend on one cultural norm)

### 4.3 Judgement Dimension Mapping

Identify what "good judgement" looks like for this construct. Map the dimensions the SJT will measure:

| Judgement dimension | What it captures | How it appears in response options |
|---|---|---|
| **Stakeholder awareness** | Considers who is affected | Options that account for vs. ignore stakeholder impact |
| **Time horizon** | Balances short vs. long-term | Options that sacrifice now for later vs. grab quick wins |
| **Information use** | Gathers data before acting vs. acts on assumptions | Options that seek information vs. jump to conclusions |
| **Risk calibration** | Appropriate risk tolerance | Options that are too cautious vs. too reckless vs. calibrated |
| **Interpersonal sensitivity** | Handles people considerations | Options that consider relationships vs. ignore them |

## 5. Scenario Design Rules

### Rule 1: Create Realistic Dilemmas, Not Puzzles

Scenarios should describe situations that actually occur in the workplace, not abstract problems. The respondent should recognise the situation from their own experience.

**Bad scenario (abstract puzzle):**
> "You need to allocate resources between two competing projects. Project A has higher expected value but higher risk. Project B has lower value but is certain to succeed."

**Good scenario (realistic dilemma):**
> "Your team has been asked to take on an urgent project from a senior executive. Completing it by the deadline would require pulling two people off a project you committed to delivering for another department next week. The other department has already communicated the timeline to their client."

### Rule 2: Provide Sufficient Context

The scenario must include enough information for the respondent to evaluate the options. Missing context forces respondents to make assumptions, introducing noise.

**Include:**
- Who is involved (roles, not names)
- What the situation is
- What makes it difficult (the tension or trade-off)
- Any relevant constraints (time, resources, authority)
- Enough detail to differentiate between response options

**Do not include:**
- Irrelevant details that distract
- Emotional framing that steers toward an answer ("your team is demoralised and desperate")
- Information that makes one option obviously correct

### Rule 3: Keep Scenarios Between 50-120 Words

Too short and the scenario lacks context. Too long and respondents lose focus. Aim for enough detail to create a genuine dilemma without overwhelming.

### Rule 4: Avoid "Loaded" Scenarios

The scenario should not telegraph the correct answer through its framing.

| Loaded (telegraphs answer) | Neutral (genuine dilemma) |
|---|---|
| "A team member has been consistently underperforming despite multiple conversations..." (implies escalation) | "A team member's output has declined over the past month. They recently took on additional responsibilities in another project." |
| "Your manager makes an unreasonable request..." (implies pushback) | "Your manager asks you to take on a task that would require deprioritising a commitment you made to another team." |

### Rule 5: Vary Scenario Difficulty

Like self-report items, SJT scenarios should vary in complexity:

| Level | Characteristics | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Straightforward** | Clear trade-off, moderate stakes, most competent people get it right | Balancing two requests with different deadlines |
| **Complex** | Multiple stakeholders, competing values, higher stakes | Handling a situation where the "right" thing to do may upset a senior stakeholder |
| **Ambiguous** | Incomplete information, no clearly superior option, high stakes | Making a call under time pressure with conflicting advice from trusted colleagues |

Target: ~25% straightforward, ~50% complex, ~25% ambiguous.

### Rule 6: One Construct Per Scenario

Each scenario should primarily measure one construct or judgement dimension. If a scenario requires good judgement across three unrelated dimensions, the scoring becomes uninterpretable.

## 6. Response Option Design Rules

Response options are the most technically demanding part of SJT design. Poor options create measurement noise, obvious answers, or uninterpretable scores.

### Rule 1: All Options Must Be Plausible

Every option must be something a reasonable person could genuinely choose. If an option is obviously bad ("ignore the situation and hope it resolves"), no one selects it and it wastes space.

**The plausibility test:** Would at least 10% of the target population genuinely consider this the best option? If not, rewrite.

### Rule 2: Options Should Span the Effectiveness Continuum

Typically 4 options per scenario, distributed roughly:
- 1 most effective action
- 1-2 moderately effective actions
- 1 least effective (but still plausible) action

The key: the least effective option should still be a recognisable workplace behaviour, not an absurd choice.

### Rule 3: Options Must Be Mutually Exclusive

Each option should describe a distinct course of action. If two options are variations of the same approach, respondents cannot meaningfully distinguish them.

### Rule 4: Options Should Differ on the Target Construct

The difference between options should reflect the judgement dimension being measured, not incidental details.

**Bad options (differ on incidental details):**
- A. Email the stakeholder on Monday
- B. Email the stakeholder on Tuesday
- C. Call the stakeholder on Monday
- D. Schedule a meeting for Wednesday

**Good options (differ on judgement):**
- A. Proceed with the original plan and update stakeholders after the fact
- B. Consult the affected stakeholders before making a change
- C. Escalate the decision to your manager
- D. Delay the decision until you have more information

### Rule 5: Match Option Length

Options of similar length prevent respondents from using length as a cue. If the "best" answer is consistently the longest option, test-savvy respondents exploit this.

### Rule 6: Avoid "All of the Above" Logic

Do not include options that combine other options ("Do A, then B"), as these are typically rated as "best" by default (more action = better impression).

### Rule 7: Write Options in Parallel Structure

All options should follow the same grammatical structure. Inconsistent structure creates unintended cues.

## 7. Response Instruction Formats

Three main approaches, each with different psychometric properties:

### Most/Least Effective (Recommended Default)

> "For each scenario, select the action you believe would be MOST effective and the action you believe would be LEAST effective."

**Advantages:** Forces discrimination between options; produces more data per scenario; moderate faking resistance.
**Disadvantages:** Slightly more cognitively demanding; requires clear instructions.
**Scoring:** Most effective choice + least effective choice each contribute to the score.

### Best Single Choice

> "For each scenario, select the action you believe would be most effective."

**Advantages:** Simple to understand; familiar format; quick to complete.
**Disadvantages:** Less data per scenario; respondents may struggle when two options seem equally good.
**Scoring:** Score based on alignment with expert-keyed best answer.

### Rank Order

> "Rank all four actions from most effective (1) to least effective (4)."

**Advantages:** Maximum data per scenario; reveals fine-grained judgement.
**Disadvantages:** Most cognitively demanding; takes longest; can produce response fatigue.
**Scoring:** Correlation between respondent ranking and expert ranking (e.g. Spearman's rho per scenario, averaged).

**Recommendation:** Default to most/least effective. Use best single choice for lower reading levels or when assessment time is constrained. Use rank order only when fine-grained discrimination is essential and the population can handle the cognitive load.

## 8. Scoring Approaches

SJT scoring requires a keying framework — unlike Likert items, there is no simple "higher = better" direction. The scoring key must be established before operational use.

### Expert Consensus Keying (Recommended for Initial Development)

1. Assemble 5-10 subject matter experts (SMEs) familiar with the target role
2. Have each SME independently complete the SJT with rationale
3. Identify consensus best and worst options (typically 70%+ agreement threshold)
4. Scenarios without clear expert consensus may need revision

### Empirical Keying (Gold Standard, Requires Data)

1. Administer the SJT to a large sample
2. Correlate each option choice with an external criterion (performance ratings, outcomes)
3. Options chosen by high performers get higher weights
4. Requires criterion data and sufficient sample size

### Rational Keying (Minimum Viable, Use With Caution)

1. The item designer determines best/worst options based on the construct definition
2. Acceptable for development contexts and piloting
3. Must be validated by SME consensus or empirical data before operational use

**Always note the scoring approach in the final deliverable and flag that empirical validation is required.**

## 9. SJT Item Pool Format

Present each SJT scenario using this structure:

### Scenario [#]: [Brief Label]

**Construct measured:** [Name]
**Judgement dimension:** [Which dimension from 4.3]
**Difficulty:** Straightforward / Complex / Ambiguous
**Trade-off:** [What tension the scenario presents]

**Scenario text:**
> [50-120 word scenario description]

**Response options:**

| Option | Action | Effectiveness | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | [Action description] | Most / Moderate / Least | [Why this level of effectiveness] |
| B | [Action description] | Most / Moderate / Least | [Why] |
| C | [Action description] | Most / Moderate / Least | [Why] |
| D | [Action description] | Most / Moderate / Least | [Why] |

**Scoring notes:** [Any concerns about option plausibility, cultural bias, or construct contamination]

**Important:** The rationale column is for the assessment designer, not the respondent. It documents why each option is keyed at its effectiveness level.

## 10. SJT-Specific Quality Review

### Individual Scenario Checks

| Check | Question | Action if failed |
|---|---|---|
| **Realistic dilemma** | Would the target population recognise this situation? | Ground in real workplace examples |
| **Genuine trade-off** | Is there a real tension, or is one option obviously best? | Strengthen the trade-off or revise options |
| **All options plausible** | Would at least 10% of the population choose each? | Replace implausible options |
| **Options discriminate** | Do options differ on the target construct, not incidentals? | Rewrite to differentiate on judgement quality |
| **Balanced length** | Are options similar in length? | Equalise word counts |
| **No loaded framing** | Does the scenario avoid telegraphing the answer? | Neutralise emotional or leading language |
| **Cultural neutrality** | Does the "right" answer assume a specific cultural norm? | Rewrite for cultural flexibility |
| **Authority appropriate** | Could the target population take all described actions? | Adjust actions to the population's authority level |
| **Single construct** | Does the scenario primarily measure one construct? | Simplify or split the scenario |

### Pool-Level Checks

| Check | Target |
|---|---|
| **Difficulty distribution** | ~25% straightforward, ~50% complex, ~25% ambiguous |
| **Trade-off coverage** | Each identified trade-off appears in at least 1-2 scenarios |
| **Judgement dimension coverage** | All mapped dimensions represented |
| **Scenario variety** | No two scenarios describe the same situation from different angles |
| **Total assessment time** | 6-12 scenarios per construct; estimate 2-3 minutes per scenario |
| **Scoring feasibility** | Expert consensus achievable for all scenarios |

## 11. Common SJT Design Failures

| Failure | Example | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Obvious best answer** | Options include "gather all information, consult stakeholders, and make a careful decision" | No discrimination; everyone picks the comprehensive option | Make all options involve trade-offs; remove the "do everything" option |
| **Absurd worst answer** | Option: "Ignore the problem and go on holiday" | No one selects it; wastes an option slot | Replace with a recognisable but less effective workplace behaviour |
| **All options equivalent** | Four variations of "talk to the person" with minor differences | Cannot score meaningfully; options don't span effectiveness | Ensure options represent genuinely different approaches |
| **Scenario too vague** | "A colleague disagrees with you. What do you do?" | Not enough context; respondents project their own scenario | Add who, what, stakes, constraints |
| **Scenario too long** | 200+ word scenario with complex backstory | Cognitive overload; respondents skim and miss key details | Edit to 50-120 words; keep only decision-relevant information |
| **Cultural bias in keying** | "Best" answer requires confrontation in a culture that values indirect communication | Systematic bias against certain groups | Ensure effectiveness keying holds across cultures; consult diverse SMEs |
| **"More is better" bias** | Best option involves the most actions; worst involves fewest | Respondents learn to pick the most active option | Include options where restraint or inaction is the best judgement |
| **Double-barrelled options** | "Speak to the team member and escalate to HR" | Two actions bundled; respondent may agree with one but not the other | Each option = one clear course of action |
